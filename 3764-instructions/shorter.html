Instructions: an intro

The point of this lecture is to begin to move you from a writer mindset to a user mindset.

Part 1: The illusion of explanatory depth

First, how well would you say you understand how a bicycle works? Just a simple, single-gear bike. Rate your level of understanding from 1 (no understanding) to 10 (certainty). Keep that number in mind.

Now copy the picture of the bike on a piece of paper (or on your computer). Complete the bike by drawing the frame, gear, and pedals where they belong on the bike.

Now, having completed your sketch, rate your understanding from 1 to 10 again. If you're second-guessing yourself, pick a lower number. If you feel even more confident, pick a higher number.

Now try circling the correct configuration below.

The correct answers are

  1. 1
  2. 2
  3. 4

Now rate your understanding from 1 to 10 once more.

Note how confidence decreased by half a point after students had to actually draw the bike. That overconfidence is what we call

The illusion of explanatory depth

The illusion of explanatory depth means that people think they know how something works until they have to explain how it works.

If writing instructions feels easy, beware: You may not be thinking like a user.

Part 2: The Curse of Knowledge

The curse of knowledge means that when you deeply understand something, you will have a very difficult time imagining how other people will struggle to understand the same thing.

I thought the red button was pretty obvious, but

  1. I'd been using the term publish, while the service uses the word send
  2. Some people did push the button but then didn't immediately receive notification that their letter had sent

What is obvious to you will NOT necessarily be obvious to users.

Part 3: Problems of scale

Let's play some QWOP

People who might need instructions on how to walk:

  • Amputees
  • People injured in accidents
  • Elderly people
  • People with chronic foot, leg, hip, and/or back pain

Which of these people do we want to help?

Murphy's Law

Given enough trials, anything that can go wrong will eventually go wrong. Any flaw will be exposed.

1-(1-p)^n

Perfection is unattainable. The optimal is the best you can hope for.

Part 4: Acheiving the optimal

(Last part, I promise!)

1. Know your audience

  • Never forget who your users are and how they differ from you
  • People who aren't your users generally don't matter. There is no "general public"
  • Use the rhetorical analysis and personas to get to know your users

2. Ask what could go wrong

Every sentence, every word can potentially cause problems. Here are just a few:

  • Do you have users who may not speak English as their primary language?
  • Do you have users who may not understand technical terms?
  • Do you have users who will be following your instructions on a phone?

3. Test your instructions

The ONLY way to truly know if your instructions work optimally is to find users, ask them to use your instructions, and get feedback.

If you can't do that, then at least test against the concepts outlined above:

  • The illusion of explanatory depth
    Explain your task to a friend and note where you have trouble explaining
  • The curse of knowledge
    You are most likely more capable than your users. Look for uncommon words, complicated phrasings--never forget your bottom quintile of users
  • Problems of scale
    Imagine as many common use cases as possible. More on this later
